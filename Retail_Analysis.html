Retail Sales: Food and Beverage Stores

Introduction

Understanding consumer spending patterns is essential for retailers, policymakers, and investors. In this project, I analyzed monthly U.S. retail sales data for food and beverage stores, sourced from the U.S. Census Bureau’s Monthly Retail Trade Survey (via FRED).

The goal of this analysis was to apply time series decomposition techniques to uncover the main components driving sales: the long-term trend, recurring seasonal patterns, and short-term irregular fluctuations. By breaking the data into these pieces, we can better understand how sales evolve over time and what forces shape them.

Methodology

To better understand the retail sales data, I used a technique called time series decomposition. This approach breaks down a sequence of observations, such as monthly sales, into three core components:

  • Trend – the long-term direction of sales over time, showing whether spending is generally increasing, decreasing, or stable.

  • Seasonality – recurring patterns that repeat at regular intervals, such as higher spending during the holiday season.

  • Residuals (or irregular component) – the random ups and downs that cannot be explained by trend or seasonality.

For this project, I used statistical tools in R to perform the decomposition and create visualizations of each component. This made it possible to see not only the overall sales growth but also the seasonal cycles and the unpredictable fluctuations.

Choice of Model

In breaking down the sales data, I chose to use a multiplicative model rather than an additive one. The main reason is that the size of the seasonal swings in food and beverage sales tends to grow as overall sales grow. For example, the holiday surge in recent years is larger in dollar terms than it was twenty years ago. An additive model would assume the seasonal pattern is the same size every year, but that does not match the reality of this dataset.

The multiplicative approach allows the seasonal ups and downs to scale with the overall level of sales. This means the model captures the fact that when sales are higher, seasonal peaks and dips are also more pronounced. In short, it provides a more realistic picture of how trend, seasonality, and random changes interact in this market.

The long-term trend in the data shows a steady increase in sales at food and beverage stores, measured in millions of U.S. dollars. Month after month and year after year, the overall direction points upward. This suggests that consumer spending in this category has grown consistently, which may be driven by factors such as population growth, changing consumption habits, and broader economic expansion.

It is important to note, however, that the sales figures are not adjusted for inflation. This means part of the upward trend may reflect higher prices over time rather than purely an increase in the volume of goods sold. Even so, the sustained rise highlights a strong and persistent demand for food and beverage products.

The seasonal component of retail food and beverage sales makes it clear that spending follows predictable cycles that repeat each year. The most prominent feature is the sharp rise in sales during November and December, which reflects the holiday season when households increase purchases for celebrations and gatherings. This surge is immediately followed by the lowest point of the year in January and February, when consumers typically scale back spending after the holidays.

Beyond the holiday effect, the data also shows smaller but consistent fluctuations throughout the year. Sales often climb again in late spring and early summer, likely linked to graduations, vacations, and more frequent social gatherings that drive demand for food and beverages. By late summer, spending tends to dip before beginning its climb into the fall and eventually peaking again during the holidays.

Taken together, these repeating patterns demonstrate that seasonality in food and beverage sales is not limited to the holiday rush. Instead, the industry experiences multiple cycles within a year, each of which can be anticipated and factored into business planning.

Disclaimer: The seasonal graph is limited to a shorter portion of the timeline for clarity. These repeating seasonal patterns, however, are consistent across the entire dataset.

After removing the long-term trend and the repeating seasonal cycles, what remains are the random fluctuations in sales. These represent the short-term ups and downs that cannot be explained by predictable patterns. For the most part, these random movements are small and balanced around the average, showing the kind of everyday noise you would expect in retail data.

However, there are also noticeable spikes and dips at certain points in history. One of the most striking examples occurs in 2020, when sales surged and then dropped sharply as the COVID-19 pandemic disrupted consumer behavior. Events like this stand out in the random component because they do not follow the usual seasonal or long-term patterns.

These irregular movements are important to recognize because they remind us that not all changes in sales are predictable. While businesses can plan around steady growth and seasonal cycles, sudden shocks whether from economic crises, public health events, or other unforeseen circumstances can still create large temporary swings in demand.

Conclusion

The decomposition of retail food and beverage sales highlights three key insights: sales have followed a steady upward trend over time, they display strong and recurring seasonal patterns, and they are occasionally disrupted by random shocks such as the COVID-19 pandemic. Together, these components show that while parts of consumer spending in this sector is predictable or rather follows a pattern, businesses must remain flexible to respond to unexpected events.

Limitations and Considerations

While decomposition is a powerful tool for understanding time series data, it is important to recognize its constraints. The trend, seasonality, and random components shown in this report are statistical estimates, not exact representations of reality. For example, the trend line smooths over short-term fluctuations to highlight the broader direction of sales, but the actual path of consumer spending may not move quite as smoothly as the plotted trend suggests. Similarly, the seasonal and random components are shaped by the model’s assumptions, which means they simplify some of the complexity of real-world behavior.

These limitations do not reduce the value of the analysis on the contrary, they help make the data easier to interpret. However, they serve as a reminder that the results should be viewed as approximations of underlying patterns rather than precise forecasts of future behavior.
